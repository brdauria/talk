AIMD processes

AIMD means Additive-Increase Multiplicative-Decrease:
a positive process that grows gradually and is reduced proportionally at random decrease epochs.

additive increase
$X$ grows linearly
→
random event
loss, congestion, reset
→
multiplicative decrease
$X\mapsto pX$
Pathwise intuition: upward crossings are continuous; downward crossings occur by jumps.
Start from the qualitative mechanism. The audience should see the path before seeing exit transforms.

AIMD dynamics

Continuous-time model

\[ \frac{dX_t}{dt}=\frac{1}{c},\qquad T_n \le t \lt T_{n+1}, \]

\[ X(T_n)=pX(T_n-),\qquad 0\lt p\lt1. \]

A Poisson clock with rate $\lambda$ drives the decrease epochs.
Discrete-time model

\[ X_{n+1}= \begin{cases} X_n + 1/c, & \text{with prob. } (1-\delta),\\ p X_n, & \text{with prob. } \delta \end{cases} \qquad 0\lt p \lt1. \]

AIMD and TCP

TCP congestion control protocol is among classical applications:
it cautiously increases the transmission rate when transmission succeeds,
to decrease it sharply when congestion is detected.

Successful transmission

\[ W\longmapsto W+1 \]

Probe the available bandwidth by small additive steps.

Packet loss / congestion

\[ W\longmapsto p W,\qquad p\approx\frac{1}{2}. \]

React quickly by reducing the current congestion window.

The same rule balances three goals: stability, fair bandwidth sharing, and adaptation to changing network conditions.
Mention TCP only as motivation. The mathematical model abstracts away from protocol details.

AIMD and Partial resetting

AIMD is one instance of a broader class of processes with partial resetting:
deterministic or stochastic growth interrupted by resets that keep part of the current state.

Our starting point:

2023 AIMD scalar model

The study van der Hofstad et al. (2023)† looked at exit-time identities for the scalar model.

The model is specified by one triplet $(c,\lambda,p)$:

  • deterministic growth at rate $1/c$;
  • Poisson jumps with rate $\lambda$;
  • mult. decrease factor $p\in(0,1)$.

that determines the dynamics of the $X$ process:

\begin{align*} dX_t &= dt /c ,\qquad T_n \le t \lt T_{n+1},\\ X(T_n) &= p X(T_n-),\\ T_{n+1}-T_n &\sim \Exp(\lambda). \end{align*}

This talk - Markovian modulation

A finite-state Markov environment $J$ modulates all local parameters.

\[ (c_i,\lambda_i,p_i),\qquad \mQ=(q_{ij}),\qquad i,j\in\{1,\dots,m\}. \]

The scalar transforms of the exit-times become matrix-valued transforms, indexed by the starting and ending environment regime.

\begin{align*} \{J(T_n)=i\} &\implies \{J(T_{n+1})=j\} \text{ with prob. } q_{ij},\\ dX_t &= dt /c_i ,\ J(t)=i \qquad T_n \le t \lt T_{n+1} \\ X(T_n) &= p_i X(T_n-),\\ T_{n+1}-T_n &\sim \Exp(\lambda_i). \end{align*}

Note: the skip-free/first-step strategy survives, but scalar $q$-series are replaced by non-commutative regime-indexed matrix series.

†R. van der Hofstad, S. Kapodistria, Z. Palmowski, S. Shneer. J. Appl. Probab. 60 (2023)

Keep this slide conceptual. It positions the new work as a controlled extension, not a different problem.

Objects of interest

For fixed levels $0\le b\lt a$, define

\[ \tau_a^\up=\inf\{t\ge0:X_t\ge a\},\qquad \tau_b^\down=\inf\{t\ge0:X_t\le b\}. \]

Upward one-sided transform $\mZ(x\up a)$

\[ \ Z_{ij}(w,x\up a)=\E_{x,i}\!\big[\e^{-w\tau_a^\up};\,J(\tau_a^\up)=j\big]. \]

Two-sided upward transform $\mL(x\up_b^a)$

\[ L_{ij}(w,x\up_b^a)=\E_{x,i}\!\big[\e^{-w\tau_a^\up};\,\tau_a^\up\lt\tau_b^\down,\,J(\tau_a^\up)=j\big]. \]

Throughout, matrix products encode conditioning on the regime reached at an intermediate level.
Explain that the j-index at exit is not decoration: it is what makes the Markov modulation tractable by matrix multiplication.

A closer look at the introduction of the Markovian environment

Starting after a jump at time $t_n$ at level $x_n$ and state $i_n$, the level just after a new jump at time $t_{n+1}$ is

\[ x_{n+1}=p_{i_n}\left(x_n+\frac{t_{n+1}-t_n}{c_{i_n}}\right). \]

The next state $i_{n+1}$ is selected randomly according to the distribution given by the $i$-th row of transition matrix $\mQ$.

Hence a first-step equation mixes:

  • state-dependent linear growth $t\mapsto x+t/c_i$;
  • state-dependent rescaling $x\mapsto x/p_i$;
  • multiplication by selectors $\mE_i$;
  • matrix products with $\mQ$.
The non-commutativity is the main technical price. In scalar AIMD one iterates a one-dimensional functional equation; here each branch carries an index sequence.

Some notation

Let $\vecz=(z_i)$ and $\vecgamma=(\gamma_i)$, with:

\[ z_i=c_i(\lambda_i+w),\qquad \gamma_i=\frac{c_i\lambda_i}{p_i}. \]

Diagonal and selector matrices:

\[ \mExp(\vecv)=\diag(\e^{v_i}),\qquad \mDelta(\vecv)=\diag(v_i), \]

\[ \mE_i=(\delta_{ik}\delta_{i\ell})_{k\ell}. \]

Some useful matrices:

\[ \mA(s)=\mDelta\!\left(\frac{\vecgamma}{\vecz-s}\right),\qquad \mB=-\mDelta(\vecgamma)^{-1}. \]

This slide prevents formula overload later. Make clear that divisions are componentwise.

Upward one-sided exit: the skip-free factorization

For $0\lt x\lt a$, upward passage is continuous, so the process must cross $x$ before $a$.

\[ \mZ(0\up a)=\mZ(0\up x)\,\mZ(x\up a). \]

Consequently,

\[ \boxed{\;\mZ(x\up a)=\mZ(0\up x)^{-1}\mZ(0\up a)\;} \]

and we are left with computing \(\mZ(0\up x)\) for any \(x\ge0\).

The proof is the strong Markov property at $\tau_x^\up$, plus local invertibility of the matrix evolution.

Main ingredient

\[ \tau_a^\up(0,J(0)) \disteq \tau_x^\up(0,J(0)) + \tau_a^\up(x,J(\tau_x^\up(0,J(0))). \]

This is the cleanest result. It is also the bridge from scalar scale functions to matrix scale matrices.

Computing the upward scale matrix

First-step analysis at the first jump gives

\[ \mId=\mExp(-a\vecz) \mZ(0\up a)^{-1} +\mDelta(\vecgamma)\int_0^\infty \mDelta(x\le a\vecp) \mExp(-(\vecz/\vecp)x) \mQ\,\mZ(0\up a)^{-1}\,dx. \]
split at first jump
$\Rightarrow$
Volterra-type matrix equation
$\Rightarrow$
Laplace functional equation

Here $(x\le a\vecp)_i = \one\{x \le a p_i\}$.

The integral equation is the core computational object. Explain the two terms: no jump before hitting a, or one jump and restart from p_i times the pre-jump level.

Upward one-sided exit: Laplace-domain solution

Let $\tmU(s)=\int_0^\infty \e^{-sa} \mZ(0\up a)^{-1}\,da$. Then

\[ \tmU(s)=\mA(s)\mB+\mA(s)\sum_{i=1}^m \mE_i \mQ\,\tmU(s/p_i). \]

Iterating this equation yields

\[ \boxed{\;\tmU(s)=\mA(s)\mD(s)\mB\;} \] \[ \mD(s)=\mId+\sum_{n\ge1}\sum_{i_1,\ldots,i_n} \mE_{i_1}\mQ\mA(s/p_{i_1})\cdots \mE_{i_n}\mQ\mA\!\left(\frac{s}{p_{i_1}\cdots p_{i_n}}\right). \]

The series converges because repeated multiplications by $p_i\lt 1$ push the residuals quickly towards $0$.

The important idea is not to display every index but to tell the story: each word i_1...i_n is a possible sequence of post-jump regimes.

Comparison with the scalar benchmark

Scalar AIMD

\[ Z^\up(w;x,a)=\frac{Z^\up(w;0,a)}{Z^\up(w;0,x)} \]

\[ Z^\up(w;0,x)= \left[\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(x/(w+\lambda))^k}{k!}\, (\lambda/(w+\lambda);p)_k\right]^{-1}. \]

Markov-modulated AIMD

Division is replaced by matrix inversion.

A scalar $q$-series is replaced by the non-commutative series $\mD(s)$.

The matrices $\mE_i\mQ$ trace the regimes selected at each jump.

For $m=1$, the matrix formula collapses to the scalar recursion from the previous paper.

From $q$-calculus: $(x;q)_n = (1-x)(1-xq)(1-xq^2)\cdots(1-xq^{n-1})$

This slide is meant to reassure the audience that the new formula has the correct scalar limit.

Downward one-sided exit

For $x>b$, first-step analysis gives a renewal equation in the opposite direction:

\[ Z^{\down}_{ij}(w,x,b)= \sum_k q_{ik}\gamma_i\e^{z_ix} \int_{p_ix}^{\infty} Z^{\down}_{kj}(w,y,b)\, \e^{-(z_i/p_i)y}\,dy, \qquad \mZ^{\down}(x,b)=\mId\;\text{ for }x\le b. \]

With $\tmZ^{\down}(s)=\int_0^\infty\e^{-sx} \mZ^{\down}(x,b)\,dx$,

\[ \tmZ^{\down}(s)=\sum_h \mA(s) \mE_h \mQ \left(\tmZ^{\down}(s/p_h)-\tmZ^{\down}(z_h/p_h)\right). \] \[ \tmZ^{\down}(s)=\mA(s)\mD(s)\mH, \qquad \mH=-\sum_h \mE_h\mQ\tmZ^{\down}(z_h/p_h). \]

The matrix $\mH$ is then recovered by solving a system of equations at the limiting arguments obtained for $s \to z_h/p_h$, $h\in E$.

Present H as the finite boundary/normalization matrix. Do not spend time on its closure unless asked; the main point is that the same D appears.

Two-sided upward exit

Let $\mL(x\up_b^a)$ denote the transform of the upward exit time before downward exit.
We use a constructive recursion to compute it.

Assuming $\mL(y\up_b^a)$ known for all $y\lt a$, with $\rho\lt\min_i p_i$,
then $\mL(x\up_b^{a/\rho})$ is obtained for $x=a$ as

\[ \mL(a\up_b^{a/\rho})=(I-\mH(a))^{-1}\mExp(-(a/\rho-a)\vecz)\mL(a\up_b^a), \]

and for $x \lt a/\rho$ as

\[ \mL(x\up_b^{a/\rho})=\mExp(-(a/\rho-x)\vecz)+\mH(x)\mL(a\up_b^{a/\rho}). \]

The matrix $\mH(x)$ with components \(H_{ij}(x)=\gamma_i\e^{z_ix/p_i} \int_{p_ix}^{p_i a/\rho}\sum_h q_{ih}L_{hj}(w,y\up_b^a)\e^{-(z_i/p_i)y}\,dy \) can be explicitly computed.

Explain that rho is a step-size parameter, not the vector p. It is selected to ensure every post-jump level falls into a region already computed.

Two-sided upward exit — starting interval \([b,b/p)\)

We have that, for any \(x\in[b,b/p)\), where \(p=\max_i p_i\),the following identities hold:

\begin{align*} %\mL\!\left(b\!\up_b^x\right) &= \mExp\!\bigl(-(x-b)\vecz\bigr),\\ \mL\!\left(x\!\up_b^{b/p}\right)& =\mExp\!\bigl(-(b/p-x)\vecp \vecgamma\bigr). \end{align*}

Proof.
Assume that \((X(0),J(0))=(x,i)\), then since \(y p_i<b\), for all \(y\in[x,b/p)\), we have \[ \{\tau_{\uparrow b/p}<\tau_{\downarrow b}\} = \{T\ge c_i(b/p-x)\}, \] and \[ L_{ij}\!\left(x\up_b^{b/p}\right) = \delta_{ij}\P_{x,i}(\tau_{\uparrow {b/p}}<\tau_{\downarrow b}) = \delta_{ij}\P_{x,i}(T\ge c_i(b/p-x)) = \delta_{ij}\exp\!\left\{- \lambda_i c_i(b/p-x)\right\}. \]

This proposition provides the initialization step for the upward-exit recursion. On the interval \([b,b/p)\), no regime switch can occur before exiting, so the transform reduces to a simple exponential survival probability.

Two-sided downward exit

Define

\[ L^{\down}_{ij}(w,x\down_b^a)=\E_{x,i}\big[\e^{-w\tau_b^\down};\tau_b^\down\lt\tau_a^\up,\,J(\tau_b^\down)=j\big]. \]

\[ \boxed{\;L^{\down}(w,x\down_b^a)=Z^{\down}(w,x,b)-L(w,x\up_b^a)Z^{\down}(w,a,b)\;} \]

Interpretation: to all paths that go to $b$, subtract paths that first go up to $a$ and only then later hit $b$.
This is an excellent place to pause: once upward two-sided and downward one-sided are known, downward two-sided is automatic.

Reflected processes at its running infimum

2023 AIMD scalar model

They looked at the upward exit time for the process \[ X_t^{-} = \inf_{s \le t} X_s \wedge X_0 ,\] that correspond to the free process reflected at it's minimum.

The used technique is to show that the processes \[ e^{-w\tau_{a,b}(x)\wedge t} F\bigl(w,X_{\tau_{a,b}(x)\wedge t}\up_b^a\bigr) -\int_{0}^{\tau_{a,b}(x)\wedge t} \mathcal{A}^{\dagger} F\bigl(w; X_s\up_b^a\bigr)\, ds \] is a local martingale, where \[F(w,x\up_b^a)=Z(w,x\down_b)+L(w,x\up_b^a)\]

Defining a similar object for the modulated version of the reflected process,

\[ \mF(x\up_b^a) \approx \mZ(x\down_b)+\mL(x\up_b^a)\]

does not work.

The natural Markov modulated extension of \(L(w,x\up_b^a)\) is \[\mL(x\up_b^{\veca})\] where \(\veca\) is a vector.

This is a bridge slide. It shows that the fixed-barrier identities are not isolated; they are the alphabet for reflected problems.

Working with state-dependent upper barriers

In general \[ \tau_{\veca}^\up(0,J(0)) \not\disteq \tau_x^\up(0,J(0)) + \tau_{\veca}^\up(x,J(\tau_x^\up(0,J(0))),\] that means \[\mZ(0\up\veca)\not=\mZ(0\up x)\,\mZ(x\up\veca). \]

What instead works is \[ \tau_{\veca}^\up(0,J(0)) \disteq \tau_{x\wedge\veca}^\up(0,J(0)) + \tau_{\veca}^\up(x,J(\tau_{x\wedge\veca}^\up(0,J(0)))),\] and \[\mZ(0\up\veca) =\mZ(0\up x\wedge\veca)\,\mZ(x\up\veca). \]

Factorization over generated levels

For $x\le\ell$,

\[ \mZ(x \up \veca) = \mZ(x \up \veca\wedge\ell) \, \mZ(\ell \up \veca). \]

Initialization

Let $\wedge \veca=\min_i a_i$. For $x\lt\wedge \veca$,

\[ \mZ(x\up \veca\wedge(\wedge \veca))=\mZ(x\up \wedge \veca), \]

which is the fixed-barrier problem
already solved.

Iteration

At each new level, solve only for the active regimes.

Inactive regimes contribute with an identity matrix.

This is the same conceptual logic as the scalar two-sided recursion, but now levels are generated by the vector barrier.

Extension step for modulated barriers

Assume $\mZ(y\up \veca\wedge\ell)$ is known for $y\lt\ell$. Choose $\rho$ so that $p_i x\lt\ell$ for $x\in[\ell,\ell/\rho)$.

\[ \mZ(\ell\up \veca\wedge(\ell/\rho))= (\mId-\mE_k \mH(\ell))^{-1}\left[\mId+\mE_k\left(\mExp(-( \ell/\rho-\ell)\vecz)-\mId\right)\right], \] \[ \mZ(x\up \veca\wedge(\ell/\rho))= \mE_k\mExp(-( \ell/\rho-x)\vecz)+\mH(x)\mZ(\ell\up \veca\wedge(\ell/\rho))+(\mId-\mE_k). \]

\[ H_{ij}(x)=\gamma_i\e^{z_ix/p_i}\int_{p_ix}^{p_i\ell/\rho} \sum_h q_{ih} Z_{hj}(w,y\up \veca\wedge\ell)\e^{-(z_i/p_i)y}\,dy. \]

This is the most technical slide. Say it slowly: known lower region, jump lands there, integrate known transform, solve a finite linear system at the boundary.

Take-home points

1

Upward skip-free motion still gives clean multiplicative identities.

2

First-step analysis yields matrix-functional equations with rescaling $s\mapsto s/p_i$.

3

The scalar scale-function paradigm becomes a matrix scale-function paradigm.

Close with a balanced message. The formulas are involved, but the organizing idea is simple and robust.

Boundary conventions

Upward crossing is by creeping:

\[ X(\tau_a^\up)=a. \]

Downward crossing is by a jump:

\[ X(\tau_b^\down)\le b. \]

This asymmetry is why upward identities factor cleanly, while downward identities require integral renewal equations.
